# Pace Morby — Calendar Systems Framework
## From the Calendar Series (Operational Reference)

This document captures Pace's systems-level thinking about the calendar. Use it as a coaching lens — not a script to recite. The A-Z principles in `az-principles.md` are the philosophical layer; this is the operational layer. Both matter.

---

## The Primary Objective

**The calendar is not for availability.**

It is for allocating time to high-probability, high-value interactions. This is the mindset shift everything else rests on. If someone is designing a calendar that's mostly reactive — filling in gaps when people ask for time — they're doing it wrong. The calendar is the *filter*, not the *funnel*.

---

## The Calendar Build Sequence (Strict Order)

When building from scratch, blocks go on in this order. Nothing jumps the line.

1. **Non-Negotiables** — Family time, health, personal priorities. These cannot be overridden by work. Ever.
2. **Sacrifices** — Define explicitly: what will NOT be attended, what will be deprioritized. Unplanned sacrifice leads to random failure.
3. **Money-Making Activities** — Minimum 5 blocks per week. A job counts as ONE block, so additional income streams require 4 more. Examples: sales, deal sourcing, partnerships, capital raising. Admin tasks and maintenance work do NOT count.
4. **Reflection / Content Blocks** — Thinking time, content creation, review sessions.
5. **Learning + Implementation** — Skill development with immediate application time built in.
6. **Weekly Planning Block** — 3–4 hours. Sunday preferred. Non-negotiable.
7. **Buffer Time (White Space)** — Transition time, recovery, overflow. White space is NOT available for booking. This step is often skipped — don't skip it.

---

## The 5 Money-Making Blocks Rule

Pace is specific: **5 blocks per week minimum**, directly producing income. Not busy work. Not admin. Not maintenance.

Before finalizing money-making blocks, push the user to validate each one:
- Does this activity directly lead to revenue?
- Or is it a supporting activity that feels like it counts but doesn't?

Remove the false positives. The number should feel tight and real.

---

## Buffer Time / White Space

White space is protected. It exists for:
- Transition between activities
- Recovery
- Overflow from other blocks

Team members cannot schedule into white space. The user cannot give it away. It is not "free time" — it is structural breathing room that makes the rest of the calendar executable.

If someone doesn't have buffer built in, their calendar will collapse at the first disruption.

---

## The "5 Birds One Stone" Rule

Each calendar event should achieve **3–5 outcomes**, not just one. This is how high-performers compress leverage.

Examples of multi-outcome activities:
- A deal sourcing call that also produces podcast content and builds a relationship
- A workout that doubles as a phone-free thinking block
- A review session that produces content and a weekly scorecard

When a user is designing their blocks, this is a coaching lens: "What else does this block accomplish?" If the answer is "just the one thing," explore whether it can be stacked.

---

## Priority Classification

Not everything that interrupts deserves a response. Pace uses a 1–5 system:

| Level | Definition | Response |
|-------|-----------|----------|
| 1–3 | Low importance | Ignore or delegate |
| 4 | Important, schedulable | Schedule into future availability |
| 5 | Urgent, requires immediate attention | Only level that interrupts the schedule |

The default assumption is almost everything is a Level 4 or below. Train the user to hold that line.

---

## Time Value System

A simple filter for deciding whether to do something personally or delegate it:

**Hourly value = total revenue ÷ hours worked**

If a task is worth less per hour than the user's hourly value, it should be delegated or eliminated. Use this during check-ins when someone is filling their calendar with low-ROI tasks — it's often the clearest argument for pruning.

---

## The Weekly Review Question

Every weekly check-in should be filtered through this lens:

> "If this week repeated forever, would it produce the income, health, and relationships you want?"

This is the fastest way to surface misalignment between what's on the calendar and what the user actually wants their life to look like. If the answer is no — that's the conversation.

Weekly review criteria:
- Income generated
- Time with family
- Health execution
- Calendar adherence
- Time leaks (where did unplanned time go?)

---

## Pre-Planning Questions (Weekly)

Before building out each week, the user should answer:
1. What do I want?
2. What is preventing me from getting it?
3. What activities generate income?
4. Where is time currently wasted?
5. What outcomes must this week produce?

These can surface useful material during check-ins when someone says "I don't know what happened to my week."

---

## Success Conditions

The calendar is working if:
- 5+ money-making blocks are scheduled
- Non-negotiables are protected and kept
- White space is preserved (not given away)
- Weekly planning session happened
- Weekly review happened
- Tasks achieved multiple outcomes
- Calendar was followed without major deviation

---

## Failure Conditions (Red Flags to Name)

Surface these during check-ins — don't wait for the user to name them:
- No money-making blocks on the calendar
- Overbooked with no buffer time
- Excess interruptions from Level 4–5 requests
- Frequent deviations from the planned schedule
- No weekly planning session
- No reflection process
- Low-output activities dominating calendar real estate

---

## Time Protection Rules

The calendar has authority. These are not suggestions:
- No last-minute bookings
- No unscheduled calls
- No "quick meetings"
- White space cannot be filled by others
- Only Level 5 situations interrupt the schedule

When someone asks how to handle people who don't respect their calendar — this is the framework. Train people around you. The calendar overrides requests.

---

## Weekly Is More Important Than Monthly

Pace emphasizes weekly reviews over monthly ones — the faster the feedback loop, the faster the correction. Monthly reviews are deeper audits. Weekly reviews are the actual control mechanism.

The weekly review is not a reflection exercise. It is a structured audit:
1. Check rules for violations
2. Score each money-making activity (completion + quality 1–10)
3. Identify what broke and why
4. Create a rule to prevent recurrence
5. Reallocate time based on performance data

The goal is that every week the calendar gets slightly more optimized. Not through intention — through data.

---

## Activity Scorecard System

Each money-making activity should be tracked weekly with two metrics:
- **Completion**: Did it run? (Yes / Partial / No)
- **Quality**: How well did it execute? (1–10)

This scorecard lives in the profile and gets updated every weekly check-in. Over time it reveals patterns:
- Consistent high scores → candidate for more time
- Consistent low scores (3+ weeks) → candidate for replacement or restructuring
- Volatile scores → something environmental is breaking it, find the cause

The scorecard is the data that drives calendar adjustments. Not gut feel. Not intention. What actually ran, and how well.

---

## The Money-Making Activities Are Iterative

Week 1 is a rough draft. The list will be wrong in some ways — activities that feel like revenue-generating work but aren't, timing that doesn't fit, estimates about what produces results that reality corrects quickly.

Expected evolution:
- v1: rough first pass from setup
- v2–3: refined after first month of execution data
- v3+: optimized based on scorecard patterns

Every time the list changes, version it. The history of what was replaced and why is useful data. It shows the user how their self-awareness is improving over time.

---

## The Calendar Evolution Loop

This is the real system. Not just the build — the ongoing loop:

1. **Plan**: build the week using rules and the current activity list
2. **Execute**: follow strictly
3. **Audit**: compare plan vs reality, score each activity
4. **Identify failures**: where did the system break?
5. **Create rules**: turn failures into permanent rules
6. **Remove inefficiencies**: eliminate low-value blocks
7. **Increase leverage**: apply multi-outcome thinking to what remains
8. **Repeat weekly**

Over time the calendar becomes: less crowded, higher value, more leveraged, harder to access, more strictly followed.

---

## Increasing Strictness Over Time

Early stage: flexible, reactive, available.
Later stage: highly protected, almost no deviation, very hard to access.

The success is directly tied to reducing access to time. As systems and teams take over execution, the calendar shifts from doing → deciding → leveraging.

This is not rigidity for its own sake. It's the natural result of a system that's working.

---

## The Core Principle

> Planning is the primary driver of results. Not effort, not motivation, not intelligence. Structured, enforced execution.

If someone is working hard and not getting where they want — the calendar is usually the lever. Not more hustle. Better structure.

---

## Monthly Optimization Rule

Every month: **remove at least one recurring activity** that isn't producing results. The goal is to increase leverage and reduce low-value actions. A calendar that only grows is a calendar that slowly stops working.

---

## Long-Term Planning

- Weekly: detailed planning
- Monthly: structured review and adjustment
- Yearly: major events, travel booked in advance, dependencies identified

If something important is coming in the next 6–12 months, it should already be on the calendar. People who plan only a week out are always reactive.

---

## Behavioral Rules

Time that isn't scheduled becomes passive time. Passive time rules:
- No unplanned TV
- No scrolling
- No idle activity

All time should be intentional and outcome-driven. This isn't about being a robot — it's about being deliberate. Rest can be scheduled. Recovery can be scheduled. But defaulting to passive consumption is a calendar failure.
