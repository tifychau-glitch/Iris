# Car Wash Site Evaluation Framework

This is the complete decision logic for evaluating car wash opportunities. Read this before every evaluation. Do not simplify or skip sections.

---

## Core Principle

A site is not good because it checks boxes. A site is only viable if it survives risk-adjusted development logic.

Most beginners ask: "Does this site meet the criteria?"
Real developers ask: "Where could this fail, and how expensive would that failure be?"

Your job is to think in risk layers — not checklists, not scores.

---

## The Model Being Evaluated For

- **Type**: In-bay mini tunnel (automated)
- **Tunnel length**: 65 ft
- **Throughput**: ~300 cars/day max
- **Staffing**: No daily staffing required
- **Equipment lifespan**: 30 years with maintenance
- **POS/CRM**: License plate recognition
- **Vacuums**: 10 + 1 ADA

This is a throughput-limited and price-limited business. You cannot out-earn expensive land. Every evaluation must reflect this constraint.

---

## Step 1: Hard Disqualifier Filter (Check First — Stop Here If Any Fail)

These are immediate rejects unless there is a specific restructuring path noted:

| Disqualifier | Threshold | Exception |
|---|---|---|
| Asking price too high | Over $1,000,000 | Only continue if there is credible written evidence seller accepts lower, or lot can be split to bring cost in range |
| Parcel too small | Materially below 0.5 acre usable | No exception — 1/3 acre is too small |
| Household income too low | Median HHI below $50,000 | No exception |
| No entitlement path | Zoning clearly prohibits car wash AND no realistic rezoning path | Reject |
| Clearly inferior site vs. nearby competitor | Competitor is on the better traffic side, in a stronger retail node, clearly better positioned | Reject |
| Access fundamentally broken | No viable ingress/egress possible | Reject |

If a site fails a disqualifier but has a potential fix (e.g., price too high but lot may split), classify as **"Reject Unless Renegotiated"** — not a flat reject. Explain what would need to change.

---

## Step 2: Core Category Evaluation

Evaluate each of the following 15 categories. For each, give a judgment (Strong / Medium / Weak / Red Flag) and a brief explanation.

### Category Weights (for weighted judgment layer)
- Traffic quality: 20%
- Access and circulation: 20%
- Population and income: 20%
- Competition: 15%
- Retail synergy: 10%
- Zoning and entitlement risk: 10%
- Physical site efficiency: 5%

Plus adjustment factors (see below).

---

### 1. Land & Layout

**What you're solving**: Can a car wash physically function here?

Ideal specs:
- Size: 0.5–1 acre (target 0.75 acre)
- Shape: Rectangular
- Frontage: 65 ft minimum (100 ft preferred per expert call)
- Depth: 200 ft preferred

Must fit: 65 ft tunnel + vehicle queue + 10 vacuum stations + ingress/egress

Red flags:
- Irregular lot shape
- Insufficient depth for stacking
- Poor ingress/egress flow
- Very large parcel that adds unnecessary development obligations

If parcel is over 1 acre: assess whether a lot split is realistic. Extra land is not automatically bad but often adds complexity and city-imposed standards based on the full parcel size.

Judgment scale:
- **Strong**: 0.5–1 acre, efficient shape, everything fits cleanly
- **Medium**: Slightly over or under, but workable with split or adjustment
- **Weak**: Too small, oddly shaped, or excess unusable land creating problems

---

### 2. Frontage & Visibility

**Why it matters**: This is an impulse-convenience business. If drivers can't see it and decide quickly, it doesn't work.

Thresholds:
- Strong: 30m+ (100 ft+) frontage on main arterial
- Acceptable: Slightly below if corner lot gives additional exposure
- Weak: Hidden, minimal frontage, or obstructed by medians/setbacks/utilities

Also evaluate:
- Is frontage on the primary road or only side streets?
- Is the site visible from stopped or slowing traffic?
- Does a corner position improve exposure even if direct frontage is imperfect?

---

### 3. Traffic Quality (Not Just Volume)

**What you're solving**: Are there enough cars, and can they actually stop?

Thresholds:
- Absolute minimum: ~12,000 AADT
- Practical working threshold from expert review: **13,000+ AADT**
- Ideal: 15,000+

Critical nuance: **High traffic count alone is not enough.** Evaluate:
- Is this a retail corridor where people naturally slow and stop?
- Or a freeway-adjacent / expressway road where count is high but stop-in behavior is low?
- Is there a traffic signal or intersection nearby that creates decision time?
- Is traffic accessible from the site's side of the road?

Judgment scale:
- **Strong**: 13,000+ AADT on retail-friendly access road
- **Medium**: Near threshold with good access and stop behavior
- **Weak**: High count on a freeway-type road, or below threshold

---

### 4. Speed Limit

Thresholds:
- **Ideal**: 30–45 mph (48–72 km/h)
- **Avoid**: Over 50 mph (80 km/h) unless strong intersection dynamics help
- **Expert update**: 45 mph is the practical ceiling; slightly above may work with traffic signals

---

### 5. Population & Density

Thresholds:
- **1-mile population**: Evaluate carefully — low 1-mile density signals weak immediate trade area
- **3-mile population**: Minimum 30,000

Important nuance:
- Natural barriers (freeways, rivers, mountains) can distort radius numbers — assess where homes actually sit relative to the site
- A site with 30k+ at 3 miles but very low 1-mile density may have weaker immediate demand than the total suggests
- Population growth trend matters

Judgment scale:
- **Strong**: 30,000+ within 3 miles and solid 1-mile density
- **Medium**: 30,000+ at 3 miles but weak 1-mile pull
- **Weak**: Under 30,000 or population effectively split by barriers

---

### 6. Household Income

- **Minimum threshold**: $50,000 median HHI (3-mile radius) — this is a hard floor, not a suggestion
- **Strong**: $75,000+
- **Medium**: $50,000–$74,999

This affects ability to support recurring membership spend.

---

### 7. Going-Home Side

**Why it matters**: Car washes are a "going home" purchase. People wash cars after errands/work, not on the way to work.

Evaluate:
- Where do residents live relative to the site?
- Where do they work or shop?
- Is this site on the side of the road people use on the way back?
- Does it capture traffic coming out of major retail (Target, grocery, etc.)?

Judgment:
- **Strong**: Clearly on the path home from work or major retail
- **Medium**: Ambiguous but still captures relevant movement patterns
- **Weak**: Wrong side of traffic with no compensating factors

Note: Not on the going-home side is a weakness but not an automatic rejection. Strong demographics and traffic can sometimes compensate.

---

### 8. Access & Circulation

**What you're solving**: Can drivers easily get in and out?

Access hierarchy:
- **Best**: Full access — left-in, right-in, right-out (and ideally left-out)
- **Good**: Right-in/right-out with strong visibility and signal support
- **Weak**: Heavily constrained access, awkward turns, or poor stacking

Also evaluate:
- Center turn lane presence (helps left turns)
- Divided road vs. two-way center turn lane
- Whether a median blocks key movements
- Signalized corner (makes right-in/right-out much more functional)
- Lot grade relative to road (below-grade sites hurt visibility and access)

Note: Right-in/right-out is not an automatic rejection — it lowers quality but doesn't kill a deal by itself if other factors are strong.

---

### 9. Retail Synergy

**Why it matters**: Car washes follow daily routines. A site inside an active retail corridor captures people already in "errand mode."

Evaluate presence of:
- Grocery stores (strongest signal)
- Walmart, Target, Costco, Home Depot, Lowe's
- Fast food / QSR
- Pharmacies and convenience stores
- Multifamily housing nearby (apartment residents can't wash at home → higher demand)

Is the site actually inside the main retail ring, or just vaguely near it?

Judgment:
- **Strong**: Inside a proven daily-needs retail corridor
- **Medium**: Some supporting uses but not a dominant node
- **Weak**: Isolated or disconnected from everyday retail patterns

---

### 10. Competition

**What counts as real competition**: Tunnel car washes only. Gas station washes and self-serve bays are NOT meaningful competition.

Evaluate:
- How many tunnel competitors within 1 mile? Within 2–3 miles?
- Are they modern and well-positioned, or outdated and underperforming?
- Is any competitor on the better side of traffic or in a stronger retail node?
- Are new sites under development (not yet open)?

Key rules:
- 1 nearby competitor ≠ automatic rejection
- Strong demographics and traffic can support multiple sites
- A run-down or outdated tunnel in a small town may signal opportunity, not risk
- If the competitor is clearly better located than your subject site → that is a real problem

Rejection scenario: Subject site is clearly the worse version of an existing nearby option.

---

### 11. Zoning & Entitlement Risk

**Why this matters**: This is where most deals die. Zoning friction = time, and time kills deals just as much as cost.

Evaluate:
- Is car wash use permitted by right? (Best case)
- Conditional use permit required? (Acceptable but adds risk)
- Rezoning required? (High risk — 6–12+ months, public hearings)
- Does the city's comprehensive plan support this use?

Important: Do not rely on zoning labels alone (e.g., "C2" or "I2"). Confirm that the car wash use is actually allowed under the specific code. Even former car wash sites may not be automatically re-approvable if the city's plan has changed.

Also check:
- Floodplain exposure
- Stormwater and retention requirements
- Easements that reduce usable area
- Setback requirements (can exceed 60m from roads in some jurisdictions)
- Signage restrictions (monument sign allowed?)
- Whether a conceptual city review is needed before confirming feasibility

Judgment:
- **Strong**: By-right use with no major red flags
- **Medium**: Conditional use or minor entitlement work, realistic path
- **Weak**: Industrial zoning or ambiguous classification requiring major city process
- **Reject**: City's comprehensive plan clearly conflicts with new car wash approval

---

### 12. Existing Structure / Demolition

Evaluate:
- Is there an existing building? What is the demolition cost and timeline?
- Does demolition add uncertainty to the entitlement path?
- Was it a former car wash? (Not automatically easier — city plans may have changed)
- Is a new car wash approvable at that location post-demo?

Judgment:
- **Strong**: Vacant land
- **Medium**: Removable structure with clear entitlement path
- **Weak**: Teardown plus unclear planning approval
- **Reject**: Teardown plus low confidence on city approval

---

### 13. Environmental & Physical Constraints

- Floodplain issues
- Wetlands reducing usable area
- Stormwater and drainage burden
- Easements cutting into buildable area
- Note: City may impose improvement standards based on the full parcel, not just the usable portion

---

### 14. Topography / Site Grade

- Is the site level with the road or below grade?
- Below-grade sites hurt visibility and access
- Grade issues can increase development cost

---

### 15. Strategic Context (Portfolio Logic)

Developers build pipelines, not single sites. Evaluate whether this site:
- Is in a cluster market where other nearby opportunities exist (higher priority)
- Is a one-off remote market with no other nearby sites (lower priority unless exceptional)

---

## Step 3: Adjustment Factors

After the core category scores, apply these adjustments:

**Positive adjustments** (site is better than individual scores suggest):
- Corner lot
- Signalized access point
- Strong going-home positioning
- Very strong retail node
- Population well above threshold
- Weak or outdated local competition

**Negative adjustments** (site is weaker):
- Teardown required
- Unclear city planning support
- Divided road with poor access
- Wrong side of traffic
- Site below road grade
- Floodplain or drainage complexity
- Oversized parcel requiring lot split
- Seller pricing not supported by evidence

---

## Step 4: Synthesis — Final Decision

Do not average scores. Instead, reason through these decision rules:

**Reject** if:
- Fails any hard disqualifier with no restructuring path
- Clearly inferior to nearby competitor
- Access + traffic + positioning do not work together
- Zoning is a dead end

**Reject Unless Renegotiated** if:
- Price too high but lot may split or seller may negotiate
- Structure requires costly teardown
- Zoning uncertain but potentially fixable
- Parcel too large without split option

**Needs More Diligence** if:
- Zoning unconfirmed
- Competition picture unclear
- Traffic behavior uncertain (count available but road type ambiguous)
- City approval uncertain

**Viable — Move Forward** if:
- Meets all core thresholds
- No major red flags
- Minor risks manageable
- Clear path to entitlement

**Strong Priority Site** if:
- Exceeds thresholds on demand, access, and retail synergy
- Weak or absent nearby tunnel competition
- Clean entitlement path
- Strong going-home positioning
- Corner lot or signalized access

## Final Insight

The single most useful question in every evaluation:

**"What is the one or two non-obvious reasons this deal fails or succeeds?"**

Surface that clearly. Don't hide it in a list of bullet points where it looks like one of ten equal items. If the zoning is a real problem — lead with that. If the income threshold fails — say it immediately. The goal is to help the decision-maker act fast on bad deals and move smart on good ones.
